* **isotropic:** means having identical physical properties in all directions.
*  **isotropic antenna:** is a theoretical, ideal antenna that radiates or receives radio wave energy equally in a perfect sphere (all directions).
*  **Isotropic Radiation Intensity:** The power radiated per unit solid angle by an isotropic antenna.

* **dBi (decibels relative to isotropic)** is a relative unit that measures the forward gain of an antenna compared to a theoretical, ideal isotropic antenna. It quantifies how effectively an antenna focuses radio energy in a single direction rather than scattering it in a perfect sphere

#### Calculation of antenna gain

<img width="743" height="486" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/00f825c4-bcef-4862-be83-f208e32d7678" />

* **dBd (decibels relative to a dipole):** is a relative unit that measures the gain of an antenna compared to a standard, real-world half-wave dipole antenna. Unlike dBi, which compares an antenna to a theoretical sphere, dBd uses a physical, standard hardware component as its reference baseline.
* **The Conversion Formula:** A standard half-wave dipole antenna naturally has a gain of 2.15 dBi. Therefore, converting between the two units is simple:
* $\(\text{Gain\ in\ dBi}= \text{Gain\ in\ dBd}+2.15\)$
* $\(\text{Gain\ in\ dBd}= \text{Gain\ in\ dBi}-2.15\)$
* **0 dBd Baseline:** Represents the performance of a standard dipole antenna. It is identical to 2.15 dBi.
* **Usage:** dBd is frequently used by manufacturers of land mobile radios, public safety networks, and television broadcast antennas.
* **Note:** If antenna gain is expressed as **dB**, assume as **dBd**

## Noise
Noise is a **random** signal. It extends in various forms across the frequency spectrum, although not always in the same amplitude

<img width="282" height="227" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/87547604-d90d-48ea-a163-ce34f4e263e3" />

### Categories of noise according to the frequency distribution
* **White noise:** White noise is the type of noise that affects all frequencies equally. It spreads up from zero frequency upwards with a flat amplitute, is like a constant value

<img width="430" height="202" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/4f2019d1-a7ad-4628-873e-4e5302879fd6" />

* **Pink noise:** Pink noise does not have a flat response. Its power density falls with increasing frequency

<img width="325" height="243" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/ac25d59d-5946-461b-a885-febdb1dcd286" />

* **Band limited noise:** We have noise in a specific band (noise can have its frequency band limited either by filters or the circuit through which it passes)

<img width="367" height="340" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/b532d6df-0837-4fd3-88bf-c8a65d941354" />

### Effect of Noise
* Variations in amplitude caused by noise can **mask out a signal**, or it can cause data errors, increasing the bit error rate
* For the best performance, obviously the signal should be as clear of the noise as possible
* In many instances, there is an acceptable level of data errors **or signals to noise ratio** against the cost involved
